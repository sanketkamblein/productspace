Background

The Task Given

The product team at Netcore was working on the Retention of the customer. As a UX designer I was approached by the Product Manager to re-design the workflow of the Email Campaigning module.

The team's hypothesis: revamp the entire product — update design patterns and build a brand that caters to Marketers, giving an experience that adds value to their strategy.

USP of the Product

  • Artificial intelligence to create strategy on audiences for better conversion rates
  • Templates for marketers to put least effort into crafting content
My Responsibility
Workflow design and final UI deliverable for the web application
Decision Boundary
Email content editor decisions sat with the Senior Designer — I owned end-to-end workflow and UI
Stakeholder Access
Worked directly with development manager and real users
Delivery Method
Design Sprint to deliver MVP within one quarter

How I Solved It

The Process

I went with a Design Sprint — creating concepts and retesting until positive feedback within the scheduled time. Deliverable went to the development team within the quarter.

1 Research & Interviews ▼

Spent 2 days for observation and made a hypothesis of the problems observed. Validated those observations through user interviews.

2 Persona Identification ▼

The persona varies according to the marketer's role in their team. Identification of personas helped project impact on customer share.

  • Marketer from Startup — Spends 3 days for a single campaign; uses most features
  • Marketer from MNC — Executive who broadcasts 10–20 campaigns a day
  • Marketer from Agencies — Cannot execute any campaign without client approval
3 Competitor Analysis ▼

Benchmarked the following competitors to understand the landscape and identify gaps:

  • Mailchimp
  • MoEngage
  • WebEngage
  • OptiMonk
  • Clevertap
4 Empathize / User Journey Mapping ▼

Spent time with marketers — executed their tasks alongside them to build empathy. Created User Journey Maps to prioritise UI issues found after validation sessions.

Affinity mapping session — post-its on wall grouping user pain points
User journey mapping on whiteboard — task flows and problem areas
5 Problem Identification ▼

Prioritised problems converted into design opportunities:

  • Aesthetic appeal of the product
  • Time consumed to broadcast a single email campaign
  • User irritation due to backtracking parameters while strategising
  • Use of multiple tools to execute one task
  • Features that increase learning curve but offer better outcomes
  • Fractured task relations and accessibility across different cases
6 Concept Generation ▼

Heatmapping for possible concepts done on paper. Worked on task identification, task relationships and their frequencies across edge cases. Concepts evaluated with stakeholders.

Widget vs Accordion — comparing layout concepts and their properties on whiteboard
Paper wireframes from design sprint — task flows laid out for review
7 Information Architecture ▼

IA wire-framing to define structure. A clickable wireframe was created to understand micro-interactions and validate task flows before moving to high-fidelity.

Task hierarchy mapped with post-its — left side task list, right side create campaign flow
Information Flow — three iterations with users, covering both Executor roles and Company admin
Clickable wireframe sketches — used to understand micro-interactions and test with users
8 Usability Testing ▼

Testing conducted with the in-house marketing team. Conversations were recorded for further evaluation with stakeholders. Three iterations were done testing with users before finalising the information flow.

Output

Final Design

The final high-fidelity design was built in Sketch and imported to Figma. A clickable IA wireframe prototype was also produced for micro-interaction validation before handoff to development.

Reflection

Learnings

  • The B2B business has a customer who expects particular functionality over aesthetic appeal or a trending UI
  • Documentation of every progress or milestone is very important
  • Being proactive doesn't mean following up with the task — it also means finding problems that aren't found by other stakeholders and making an action for the same
  • No matter what type of stakeholder — they don't understand a wireframe. It should be at least a low-fidelity mock-up

What I'd Change Now

I would try to bring greater flexibility in the UI which was not given then — particularly allowing marketers to personalise their workflow sequence without compromising system-wide consistency.

Web-Messages Campaigning — Survey Builder
Nov 2021 · 3 weeks · UX/UI Design · Netcore Smartech
Visual Design Interaction Design Design Systems
▼

It was my project at Netcore where I worked on a design with an available design system but the concept was new. A new type of web-message — Survey — was being added to Smartech because of market trends. I worked with PM Saurav Majumdar and Senior Designer Johnson Minj.

Role
UX / UI Designer
Duration
3 Weeks
Task
Add Surveys as a new campaign type for web-messages
Constraint
Must work within existing design system
The Challenges
  • Working with an existing design system for a product type never tested before
  • Multiple questions and multiple pages — components and conditional interactions had to be designed from scratch within constraints
  • Translating complex dynamic page logic for marketers — I imposed deliberate limitations to reduce cognitive load while creating surveys
The Process
1 Competitors Analysis ▼

Benchmarked the following to understand what existed:

  • Google's Form Builder
  • Survey Monkey
  • MoEngage
  • Clevertap
2 User Empathy ▼

Questions I explored to build empathy:

  • Why does a survey appearing on Google Maps seem hassle-free?
  • Why would I, as a user, convert for a survey?
  • What would I, as a marketer, like to put surveys out for?
3 Problem Identification ▼
  • Forms / surveys are supposed to be minimalist and aesthetically appealing
  • Survey campaigns are modal-sized on desktop and can have multiple questions
  • Marketers need to build dynamic surveys where answers change the UI
  • Surveys should be minimalist but still grab the user's attention
4 Concept Generation ▼

Studied different types of surveys. Made several concepts to understand what the builder needed — Ratings, Forms for research, Feedback. Concepts were created and evaluated with stakeholders.

Concept A — Heart rating scale, light theme
Concept A — Selected state (rating 5)
Concept B — Dark theme, multi-question dot-slider ratings
Concept C — Emoji rating scale, minimal dark modal
5 Final Design ▼

The final survey builder supports multi-page surveys with conditional routing — e.g. answer 1–2 on page 1 routes to page 2, answer 3–5 routes to page 3. Three theme options. Worked within the existing Smartech design system.

Final Survey Builder — desktop editor, multi-question, three theme options in panel
Conditional routing — emoji rating routes user to different follow-up pages
Page 2 — open text feedback after rating, with character limit control
Mobile modal — warm brown theme, toggle-style multi-select answers
Dark modal — checkbox-style answer layout, multi-select
Minimal dark feedback page — open text input, standalone
↗ View Figma Prototype
6 Usability Testing ▼

Testing done with the in-house marketing team. Conversations recorded for evaluation with stakeholders.

Learnings
  • Setting up a Usability Testing process for a brand-new feature type
  • Trade between flexibility of the design system and new elements — where to push boundaries vs. stay consistent

What I'd Change Now

I would have checked the impact of surveys in web pop-up campaigning before building and pushed the boundaries more for dynamic multi-page flows.
