On painting

I paint and sketch — mostly human portraits. When I sit down to draw someone, I am not observing their features. I am trying to understand what they are feeling, what they are holding back, what they cannot say.

That act of looking — really looking — is the same thing I do in research, in interviews, in every design decision. The medium changes. The practice doesn't.

On music, film & movement

I love listening to music and watching films. They shaped how I see. But at some point watching wasn't enough — I started performing. Hiphop and popping in college. The kind of movement that is precise and expressive at the same time.

I still do it, just not as often as I'd like. Life fills up. But the body remembers.

Some things are felt before they are understood.

That's what performing taught me. I try to build things that work the same way.

On things & taste

I'm not materialistic — I don't chase things. But I do notice design. A well-made object, a considered space, a typeface that earns its weight. Taste isn't about owning things, it's about paying attention.

On being an engineer who designs

I don't sound like most designers you meet — because I came in through engineering. I speak that language. I know what's being traded off when a decision gets made, and I know how to explain a design rationale without losing the room.

What I value most right now: clarity and logic. Not as constraints, but as a form of respect — for the people using what I build and the people building alongside me.

On the in-between

At IISc I started seeing what I had always sensed but couldn't name — that art, science, and design are not different fields. They are different ways of asking the same questions about the same human experience.

The language I am always looking for is the one that lives in that gap. The one we don't speak out loud but instantly recognise when we encounter it.

I am still learning how to speak it.

On IoT & physical computing

I play with Arduino and Raspberry Pi. Not as a hobby but as a way of thinking. When there's no screen, you have to ask what the product actually does.

I've built smart home accessories, industrial wearables, sensing systems. Currently working on educational toys where the learning happens in the hands.

Tangible interaction is the harder, more honest question.

If you're working somewhere at this intersection, reach out.
